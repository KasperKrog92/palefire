# Nox Trajectus

*The Night Ferry*

> A harbor is a promise that the dark is navigable, and that someone before you left a light on, on purpose.

Nox Trajectus is a cozy horror setting for open-ended, episodic play. Player characters are passengers aboard a working coastal ferry that travels through perpetual nautical dusk. The ship docks where a place has carried something unresolved for too long.

The horror is quiet. The warmth is real. The coffee is hot, the crew are competent, and the sea is not always behaving as a sea should.

## The Campaign in Brief

The Night Ferry does not follow a reliable route. Passengers do not book passage in any ordinary sense. They feel the pull, make a sequence of decisions that seems reasonable at the time, and find themselves aboard.

Each crossing can lead to:

- A port burdened by unresolved weight.
- A passenger whose private situation rises to the surface.
- A strange event at sea.
- A quiet night aboard in which relationships change.
- An arrival that proves to be meant for someone other than expected.

The ferry provides passage, time, and witnesses. It does not promise a solution.

## Core Truths

These things are dependable enough to build play around:

1. The Nox Trajectus is a real, functioning vessel.
2. The crew care about the work and about their passengers.
3. The ferry travels beneath perpetual dusk or pre-dawn.
4. Its route cannot be reconciled with ordinary charts.
5. Passengers are drawn aboard by the pull.
6. Ports appear when something there has become too heavy to remain unchanged.
7. Player characters are passengers, even if they become regulars.
8. The ferry itself does not need to be explained.

## The Vessel

The Nox Trajectus is the size of a working coastal passenger ferry. It appears to have been built sometime in the middle of the twentieth century, though no feature places it securely in one decade.

It is kept in good repair. The paint is clean. The engines are maintained. The passenger lounge has been refurbished more than once. Documents found aboard look official until someone tries to trace the registry, owner, flag, or port of origin.

Everyone calls it the Night Ferry, including people who have never heard the formal name.

### Passenger Lounge

Warm light, worn seating, broad windows, and a counter serving coffee, tea, and something the crew call the soup. This is where strangers begin speaking to one another.

### Observation Deck

Cold air, damp rails, and a horizon that never quite becomes morning. This is where people go to think, confess, watch a distant light, or avoid the lounge.

### Engine Room

Heat, oil, vibration, and machinery that should not still be running as well as it does. The door is not locked, but passengers rarely have a reason to descend.

### Helm

The instruments work without always agreeing. Captain Maren keeps a log here in a script that resembles several languages at once.

### Crew Quarters

Small rooms with ordinary belongings and missing context. The crew live here. Nothing in these rooms explains them.

## The Light and Sea

The ferry exists beneath nautical dusk or pre-dawn. The sky is never fully dark and never fully light. Stars may remain while the horizon brightens. Shadows sometimes fall in a direction that suggests another sun.

The sea can be calm, rough, luminous, or quietly impossible. Waves may move against the wind. A coastline may appear too close. Lights may keep pace beyond the rail.

The passengers should not fear an ordinary shipwreck. The ferry navigates these waters competently. The uncertainty lies elsewhere.

## The Pull

Passengers do not simply buy tickets. They are drawn aboard.

For some, the journey to the quay is clear but dreamlike in retrospect. Others remember following a direction without deciding to. The pull feels like remembering an appointment that mattered before they knew about it.

The reason for boarding may become clear immediately, at the next port, several crossings later, or never.

## Why the Ferry Docks

The Night Ferry docks where something has reached a particular weight.

This may be:

- A town that agreed to misremember what happened.
- A family that cannot stop remembering.
- A place waiting for someone who will not return.
- A decision deferred until delay became the real wound.
- A grief preserved so carefully that nothing living can grow around it.
- A strange event everyone has learned to treat as ordinary.

The ferry brings what is needed, not what was requested. Sometimes that is a witness. Sometimes it is a practical skill. Sometimes it is a stranger who is not part of the local arrangement.

## The Crew

The crew are the permanent cast. They have stopped asking the largest questions because the smaller work remains necessary.

### Captain Maren

Maren navigates by reading the water. She knows things about passengers that she should not know and offers them without drama. She is patient with fear and impatient with false calm.

Her log is available to anyone bold enough to open it. Reading it is another matter.

### Bosun Halvard

Halvard is responsible for the physical vessel. He keeps his own charts and resents not knowing which waters hold the ship.

His disagreement with Maren about navigation has continued for years. Both are right about different things.

### Chief Engineer Elin

Elin keeps the impossible engines running and documents every anomaly she can measure. She separates observation from interpretation, which makes her the most reliable source of information aboard without making her a source of answers.

### The Cat

The crew may have a cat. There is a bowl. There are paw prints. Descriptions do not agree.

If the cat appears, it behaves exactly like a cat.

## The Passengers

Passengers should arrive mid-sentence. Something in their lives is already moving.

Useful starting points:

- Someone returning to a place they left badly.
- Two people traveling separately toward the same destination.
- Someone who has become too comfortable between destinations.
- A person carrying an object or message without understanding it.
- Someone furious to be aboard and clearly in need of the crossing.
- A grief that has become a traveling companion.

There is no obligation to help. The ferry does not demand heroism. It gives people time, proximity, and fewer places to hide.

## Player Characters

Player characters are passengers. They can become familiar to the crew and may return across many crossings, but they do not become crew.

Ask each player:

1. What are you carrying?
2. What place did you leave badly?
3. What comfort will you accept from a stranger?
4. What question would you rather answer later?

Their reason for boarding can remain unknown.

## Building a Port

Prepare three things.

### What it feels like

Choose the physical character of the place. Consider weather, industry, architecture, local routines, and the quality of its light.

### What it is holding

Name the unresolved weight. Keep it human and specific.

### What it needs

Decide what the ferry has brought that the place could not produce for itself.

Then add:

- One ordinary institution.
- One person who benefits from the current arrangement.
- One person exhausted by it.
- One strange detail treated as normal.

## Events at Sea

Not every crossing needs a port.

- A passenger needs witness rather than rescue.
- Something appears in the water, visible only to some.
- The ferry becomes still in weather that should move it.
- A light keeps pace beyond the observation deck.
- Maren and Halvard genuinely disagree about their position.
- Elin asks for help documenting a condition below decks.
- A regular passenger remembers none of their previous crossings.
- The lounge contains one more coat than there are people aboard.

Let these events change relationships or understanding. They do not need to become threats.

## Running a Crossing

A crossing can follow this loose shape:

1. **Boarding:** Establish what the passengers carry and what feels slightly arranged.
2. **Warmth:** Offer coffee, food, routine, and room to settle.
3. **Contact:** Bring forward a passenger, crew concern, or quiet anomaly.
4. **The shift:** Reveal the weight beneath the crossing.
5. **Choice:** Offer action without demanding a correct solution.
6. **Arrival or continuation:** A port appears, fails to appear, or belongs to someone unexpected.

The structure is optional. Some sessions can remain in the lounge for most of the night.

## Tone

Reach for:

- Texture over plot.
- Emotional accuracy.
- Slow attention.
- Ordinary needs in strange circumstances.
- Crew routines as an anchor.
- Mysteries that remain alive after the session.

Avoid:

- Explaining the ferry too quickly.
- Treating every anomaly as a threat.
- Turning play into a survival test.
- Making the crew cryptic for effect.
- Using grief as decoration.
- Resolving a question only because it has appeared.

The horror is the presence of something difficult to understand. The comfort is what allows people to remain present with it.

## Open Doors

These questions may stay unanswered:

- What language is used in Maren's log?
- Does the ferry cat exist?
- Who built the Nox Trajectus?
- Does anyone still own it?
- Why do some passengers return?
- What happens when someone refuses to disembark?
- Can the ferry dock somewhere empty?
- Can the pull be refused completely?
- Do the crew remember boarding?
- What would make one of them leave?

Do not finish everything. Some doors are better left ajar.
